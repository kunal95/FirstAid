Fractures in a pre-hospital environment rarely need splinting. The most important focus in fracture management is to effectively treat any associated external bleeding and to perform the primary survey. Significant blood loss can occur from fractures and bleeding can be internal.
Causes
  • Severe bleeding
  • Major or multiple fractures
  • Severe burns or scalds
  • Major trauma
  • Severe diarrhoea and vomiting
  • Heart disorders
  • Severe sweating and dehydration (heat stroke)
  • Severe brain/spinal cord injury
  • Anaphylactic reaction
Signs and symptoms
  • Shortening of the limb
  • Tenderness
  • Swelling or irregularity
  • Shock like signs and symptoms
  • Crepitus (the sound of bones grinding)
  • Discolouration
  • Pain at the injury site
  • Bleeding (internal or external)
  • Open wounds with or without exposed bone ends
  • Discolouration
  • Deformity
  • The casualty reports hearing the bone break
  • Inability to move or stand
  • Shortening or rotation of the limb
Management
  • Carry out primary assessment, DRS ABCD
  • Seek medical attention (make sure EMS are en route)
  • Control any external bleeding using direct pressure and elevation if possible.
  • For a closed fracture, ice packs may be used to assist with pain relief and swelling.
  • Minimise any unnecessary movement unless for safety reasons
  • Check the circulation below the fracture site
  • If the injured limb needs to be immobilised, make use of whatever you have got at hand to do so e.g. pillows, magazines, or dressings to support the limb
  • Undertake a secondary assessment
  • Rest and reassure
